Specific titles, related objects and redundancy improve a visualization’s memorability

In this study, researchers look to determine what about a visualization attracts people’s attention and of that what information is encoded into memory.

The experiment consisted of three phases: participants were shown visualizations for 10 seconds, participants then signaled that they recognized a visualization, and wrote descriptions of what they recalled about a visualization.

The researchers concluded:

  • The most recognizable visualizations center of interest. Eye-tracking software determined that the most recognizable visualizations in the experiment all have a single focus in the center indicating quick recognition of the visualization, whereas the least recognizable visualizations have less singular fixation patterns.
  • Clear titles and text help recall the message. Across all textual elements, the title is among the most important. Specific titles reinforcing the visualizations main message often aided recall. Visualizations with non-specific titles can benefit from other explanatory texts such as paragraphs and labels. And visualizations with titles at the top/above the visualization also improved recall.
  • Recognizable images can help memorability. When objects provide ‘message redundancy’ they often improve the visualization’s memorability. However, objects unrelated or unclear to the visualization’s message can confuse or misdirect a viewer’s attention. Example of objects include photographs, pictograms, or realistic or abstract outlines of human recognizable objects in an image.
  • Redundancy helps recall and understanding. Data redundancy refers to visually encoding the data in more than one way—for example, including the quantitive values on a bar chart. Message redundancy presents the conclusion or message of the visualization in multiple ways to the viewer. Note: visualizations can include both data and message redundancy.
  • Memorable visualizations have memorable content. The least memorable visualizations “at-a-glance” are also the least recognizable even after pro-longed exposure.


Source: Beyond Memorability: Visualization Recognition and recall, Borkin, et al., 2015
